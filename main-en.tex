\title{Structural properties of semi-cutting sets}
\author{}
\date{}
\keywords{semi-cutting sets, shortest paths, graph contraction, terminal distances, geodesic transversals}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite simple undirected graph, let $T\subseteq V$, and let $F\subseteq E$. We call $F$ \emph{semi-cutting} for $T$ if $\dist{G}{u}{w}<\dist{G\setminus F}{u}{w}$ for every two distinct terminals $u,w\in T$. We prove the sharp bound $|F|\ge |T|-1$. The argument is structural: semi-cutting sets are first identified with edge transversals of terminal geodesics, and the bound then follows from a contraction step, a terminal-deletion lemma, and a lexicographic induction on $(|T|,|V|)$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

All graphs in this paper are finite, simple, and undirected. In particular, loops and parallel edges are excluded, and every contraction is understood in the simple-graph sense: loops are deleted and parallel edges are identified. For a graph $G=(V,E)$, a terminal set $T\subseteq V$, and an edge set $F\subseteq E$, we say that $F$ is \emph{semi-cutting} for $T$ if
\[
\dist{G}{u}{w}<\dist{G\setminus F}{u}{w}
\]
for every two distinct terminals $u,w\in T$, where distances take values in $\mathbb{N}_0\cup\{\infty\}$. No global connectivity assumption is imposed on $G$. Indeed, if some terminal pair already had infinite distance in $G$, then the defining inequality would read $\infty<\infty$ and could not hold.

The condition says that every terminal geodesic must lose at least one edge after the deletion of $F$. A lower bound of order $|T|$ is therefore natural. It is also nontrivial: one edge may lie on geodesics for many terminal pairs, so a direct count over pairs gives no useful control. Our main result is that every semi-cutting set satisfies $|F|\ge |T|-1$.

The bound is sharp. For $k\ge 2$, let $G$ be the path on vertices $v_0,v_1,\dots,v_{2k-2}$, let $T=\{v_0,v_2,\dots,v_{2k-2}\}$, and let $F=\bigl\{\{v_{2i},v_{2i+1}\}: 0\le i\le k-2\bigr\}$. Then $|F|=|T|-1$, and every component of $G\setminus F$ contains exactly one terminal. Hence each terminal-terminal distance is finite in $G$ and infinite in $G\setminus F$. Figure~\ref{fig:semi-cut-example} shows the case $k=3$.

The proof rests on two structural reductions, preceded by a geodesic characterization of the semi-cutting property. If a terminal $t$ is not incident with $F$, then all relevant obstructions lie outside $N[t]$, and contracting $N[t]$ into a single terminal preserves the semi-cutting property while reducing the number of vertices. If every terminal is incident with $F$, then one may delete a terminal $t$ together with one chosen incident edge $e\in F$: whenever a shortest path between surviving terminals uses $e$, the opposite subpath still contains another edge of $F$. These two alternatives exhaust the situation and lead to a lexicographic induction on $(|T|,|V|)$.

\section{Preliminaries}

Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite simple graph. A \emph{walk} from $u$ to $w$ is a sequence $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$ with $v_0=u$, $v_k=w$, and $\{v_{i-1},v_i\}\in E$ for $1\le i\le k$; its length is $\len{P}\coloneqq k$. A \emph{path} is a walk with pairwise distinct vertices. The distance $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is the minimum length of a $u$--$w$ path when such a path exists, and $\infty$ otherwise. A path of length $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is \emph{shortest}. For a path $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$, we write
\[
V(P)\coloneqq \{v_0,\dots,v_k\}, \qquad
E(P)\coloneqq \bigl\{\{v_{i-1},v_i\}: 1\le i\le k\bigr\},
\]
and, for $0\le i\le j\le k$, we put $P[v_i,v_j]\coloneqq (v_i,\dots,v_j)$.

For $F\subseteq E$, let $G\setminus F\coloneqq (V,E\setminus F)$ and
\[
V(F)\coloneqq \{v\in V:\ v\in e \text{ for some } e\in F\}.
\]
For $v\in V$, let $N(v)\coloneqq \{w\in V:\ \{v,w\}\in E\}$ and $N[v]\coloneqq N(v)\cup\{v\}$.

\begin{definition}[Contraction]\label{def:contraction}
Let $\emptyset\neq W\subseteq V$, and let $w^\ast\notin V$ be a new vertex. Define $\pi_W\colon V\to (V\setminus W)\cup\{w^\ast\}$ by $\pi_W(v)=w^\ast$ for $v\in W$ and $\pi_W(v)=v$ for $v\notin W$. The contracted graph $G/W$ has vertex set $(V\setminus W)\cup\{w^\ast\}$ and edge set
\[
E(G/W)=\bigl\{\{\pi_W(x),\pi_W(y)\}: \{x,y\}\in E,\ \pi_W(x)\neq \pi_W(y)\bigr\}.
\]
Thus $G/W$ is obtained by contracting $W$ to $w^\ast$ and then deleting loops and identifying parallel edges. If $e=\{x,y\}\in E$ and $\pi_W(x)\neq \pi_W(y)$, we write $e/W\coloneqq \{\pi_W(x),\pi_W(y)\}\in E(G/W)$. For $F\subseteq E$, we also put
\[
F/W\coloneqq \bigl\{\{\pi_W(x),\pi_W(y)\}: \{x,y\}\in F,\ \pi_W(x)\neq \pi_W(y)\bigr\}\subseteq E(G/W).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Semi-cutting set]\label{def:semi-cut}
Let $T\subseteq V$. An edge set $F\subseteq E$ is a \emph{semi-cutting set} for $T$ in $G$ if $\dist{G}{u}{w}<\dist{G\setminus F}{u}{w}$ for every two distinct vertices $u,w\in T$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Walks can be shortened to paths]\label{lem:walk-to-path}
Every walk from $u$ to $w$ contains a $u$--$w$ path of length at most the length of the walk.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If the walk has repeated vertices, choose two equal occurrences and delete the closed subwalk between them. The result is a shorter walk with the same endpoints. Iterating this operation until no repetition remains yields a path, and the length never increases.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Subpaths of shortest paths]\label{lem:subpath-shortest}
If $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$ is a shortest path in $G$ and $0\le i<j\le k$, then $P[v_i,v_j]$ is a shortest path from $v_i$ to $v_j$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $P[v_i,v_j]$ were not shortest, one could replace it by a shorter $v_i$--$v_j$ path and obtain a $v_0$--$v_k$ walk shorter than $P$. Lemma~\ref{lem:walk-to-path} would then produce a shorter $v_0$--$v_k$ path, a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Projection of walks under contraction]\label{lem:projected-walk}
Let $\emptyset\neq W\subseteq V$, and let $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$ be a walk in $G$. Apply $\pi_W$ to each vertex of $P$ and then delete every consecutive repetition. The resulting sequence is a walk in $G/W$ from $\pi_W(v_0)$ to $\pi_W(v_k)$, and its length is at most $k$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $z_i=\pi_W(v_i)$ for $0\le i\le k$. Whenever $z_{i-1}\neq z_i$, Definition~\ref{def:contraction} gives $\{z_{i-1},z_i\}\in E(G/W)$. Deleting consecutive repetitions from $(z_0,\dots,z_k)$ therefore yields a walk in $G/W$ with the same endpoints. Only deletions are performed, so the length does not increase.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Geodesic characterization]\label{lem:geodesic-characterization}
Let $X\subseteq V$ and $M\subseteq E$. Then $M$ is a semi-cutting set for $X$ in $G$ if and only if, for every two distinct vertices $u,w\in X$, the distance $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is finite and every shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$ contains an edge of $M$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Assume first that $M$ is semi-cutting for $X$. For distinct $u,w\in X$, the distance $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ cannot be infinite, because Definition~\ref{def:semi-cut} would then give $\infty<\dist{G\setminus M}{u}{w}$. If a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$ were disjoint from $M$, it would remain a $u$--$w$ path in $G\setminus M$, contradicting the strict inequality in Definition~\ref{def:semi-cut}.

Conversely, assume that every distinct $u,w\in X$ have finite distance in $G$ and that every shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$ contains an edge of $M$. Fix such a pair $u,w$. If $u$ and $w$ are disconnected in $G\setminus M$, then $\dist{G\setminus M}{u}{w}=\infty>\dist{G}{u}{w}$. Otherwise let $Q$ be a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G\setminus M$. Then $Q$ is also a $u$--$w$ path in $G$. Equality $\len{Q}=\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is impossible, for then $Q$ would be a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$ disjoint from $M$. Hence $\dist{G\setminus M}{u}{w}=\len{Q}>\dist{G}{u}{w}$, so $M$ is semi-cutting for $X$.
\end{proof}

\section{Structural Reductions}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:contraction-image-size}
Let $\emptyset\neq W\subseteq V$ and let $F\subseteq E$. Then $|F/W|\le |F|$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The map sending each edge of $F$ whose image is defined to its image in $F/W$ is surjective.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:outside-edges}
Let $t\in V$, let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex, and let $G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t]$. If $x,y\in V\setminus N[t]$ are distinct, then $\{x,y\}\in E(G^\prime)$ if and only if $\{x,y\}\in E(G)$; in that case $\{x,y\}/N[t]=\{x,y\}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $\{x,y\}\in E(G)$, then Definition~\ref{def:contraction} gives $\{x,y\}\in E(G^\prime)$ and $\{x,y\}/N[t]=\{x,y\}$. Conversely, if $\{x,y\}\in E(G^\prime)$, then some edge $\{u,v\}\in E(G)$ satisfies $\{\pi_{N[t]}(u),\pi_{N[t]}(v)\}=\{x,y\}$. Since neither endpoint is $t^\prime$, both $u$ and $v$ lie outside $N[t]$, hence $\{u,v\}=\{x,y\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:lifting-endpoint}
Let $t\in V$, let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex, and let $G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t]$. Let $z\in V\setminus N[t]$, and let $P^\prime=(t^\prime,x_1,\dots,x_k)$ be a shortest path from $t^\prime$ to $z$ in $G^\prime$. Then there exists a vertex $v\in N(t)$ such that $P=(t,v,x_1,\dots,x_k)$ is a shortest path from $t$ to $z$ in $G$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Because $P^\prime$ is a path, none of $x_1,\dots,x_k$ equals $t^\prime$; equivalently, all of them lie outside $N[t]$. The edge $\{t^\prime,x_1\}$ of $G^\prime$ therefore comes from an edge $\{v,x_1\}\in E(G)$ with $v\in N[t]$. Since $x_1\notin N[t]$, we must have $v\in N(t)$. Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges} shows that every edge $\{x_{i-1},x_i\}$ also belongs to $G$, so
\[
P=(t,v,x_1,\dots,x_k)
\]
is a $t$--$z$ path in $G$ of length $\len{P^\prime}+1$.

Now let $R=(r_0,\dots,r_\ell)$ be any $t$--$z$ path in $G$. Since $z\notin N[t]$, we have $\ell\ge 2$ and $r_1\in N(t)$. Applying Lemma~\ref{lem:projected-walk} to the tail $(r_1,\dots,r_\ell)$ gives a $t^\prime$--$z$ walk in $G^\prime$ of length at most $\ell-1$, hence a $t^\prime$--$z$ path of length at most $\ell-1$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:walk-to-path}. Therefore
\[
\dist{G^\prime}{t^\prime}{z}\le \ell-1=\len{R}-1.
\]
As $R$ was arbitrary, $\dist{G}{t}{z}\ge \dist{G^\prime}{t^\prime}{z}+1$. Since $P$ is a $t$--$z$ path,
\[
\dist{G}{t}{z}\le \len{P}=\len{P^\prime}+1=\dist{G^\prime}{t^\prime}{z}+1.
\]
Hence $\len{P}=\dist{G}{t}{z}$, and $P$ is shortest.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:contraction}
Let $T\subseteq V$ and $F\subseteq E$. Assume that $F$ is a semi-cutting set for $T$ in $G$. Let $t\in T\setminus V(F)$ and let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex. Put $G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t]$, $T^\prime\coloneqq (T\setminus\{t\})\cup\{t^\prime\}$, and $F^\prime\coloneqq F/N[t]$. Then $F^\prime$ is a semi-cutting set for $T^\prime$ in $G^\prime$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
No terminal of $T\setminus\{t\}$ lies in $N[t]$. Indeed, if $u\in T\setminus\{t\}$ belonged to $N[t]$, then $\{t,u\}$ would be the unique shortest $t$--$u$ path in $G$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} would force $\{t,u\}\in F$, contradicting $t\notin V(F)$. Thus $T\setminus\{t\}\subseteq V\setminus N[t]$, and $T^\prime$ is well defined.

Every two distinct terminals of $T^\prime$ have finite distance in $G^\prime$. If $u,w\in T\setminus\{t\}$, take a $u$--$w$ path in $G$; if one terminal is $t^\prime$ and the other is $z\in T\setminus\{t\}$, take a $t$--$z$ path in $G$. In both cases, Lemmas~\ref{lem:projected-walk} and~\ref{lem:walk-to-path} produce a path in $G^\prime$ joining the corresponding terminals. By Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization}, it remains to show that every shortest path in $G^\prime$ between distinct terminals of $T^\prime$ meets $F^\prime$.

Let $u,w\in T^\prime$ be distinct, and let $P^\prime$ be a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G^\prime$.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 1: $t^\prime\notin V(P^\prime)$.}
Then every vertex of $P^\prime$ lies outside $N[t]$, so $P^\prime$ is also a path in $G$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges}. It is shortest in $G$: otherwise a shorter $u$--$w$ path in $G$ would project, by Lemmas~\ref{lem:projected-walk} and~\ref{lem:walk-to-path}, to a shorter $u$--$w$ path in $G^\prime$. Since now $u,w\in T\setminus\{t\}\subseteq T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} yields an edge $g\in E(P^\prime)\cap F$. Both ends of $g$ lie outside $N[t]$, hence $g/N[t]=g\in F^\prime$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges}.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 2: $t^\prime\in V(P^\prime)$.}
Because $P^\prime$ is a path, the vertex $t^\prime$ appears on it exactly once. By symmetry, write $P^\prime=(y_0,\dots,y_m)$ with $y_r=t^\prime$ for some $r<m$. Put $z\coloneqq y_m\in T^\prime\setminus\{t^\prime\}=T\setminus\{t\}$ and $Q^\prime\coloneqq P^\prime[y_r,y_m]$. The vertices $y_{r+1},\dots,y_m$ all lie outside $N[t]$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:subpath-shortest}, $Q^\prime$ is a shortest $t^\prime$--$z$ path in $G^\prime$. Lemma~\ref{lem:lifting-endpoint} therefore provides a vertex $v\in N(t)$ such that
\[
Q=(t,v,y_{r+1},\dots,y_m)
\]
is a shortest $t$--$z$ path in $G$; see Figure~\ref{fig:contraction}.

Since $t,z\in T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} gives $E(Q)\cap F\neq\emptyset$. Its first edge $\{t,v\}$ does not belong to $F$ because $t\notin V(F)$. Hence some edge $f\in E(Q)\cap F$ lies after the first step of $Q$. If $f=\{v,y_{r+1}\}$, then $f/N[t]=\{t^\prime,y_{r+1}\}$ is the first edge of $Q^\prime$. Otherwise $f=\{y_i,y_{i+1}\}$ for some $i\in\{r+1,\dots,m-1\}$, and both endpoints of $f$ lie outside $N[t]$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges} gives $f/N[t]=f\in E(Q^\prime)$. In either case, $E(P^\prime)\cap F^\prime\neq\emptyset$.

Thus every shortest path in $G^\prime$ between distinct terminals of $T^\prime$ meets $F^\prime$. Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Deleting one terminal together with one incident edge]\label{lem:terminal-deletion}
Let $T\subseteq V$ and $F\subseteq E$. Assume that $F$ is a semi-cutting set for $T$ in $G$. Let $t\in T$, and let $e\in F$ be an edge incident with $t$. Put $T^-\coloneqq T\setminus\{t\}$ and $F^-\coloneqq F\setminus\{e\}$. Then $F^-$ is a semi-cutting set for $T^-$ in $G$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $|T^-|\le 1$, there is nothing to prove. Assume $|T^-|\ge 2$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization}, every two distinct vertices of $T^-$ have finite distance in $G$. It is therefore enough to show that every shortest path between two distinct vertices of $T^-$ contains an edge of $F^-$.

Take distinct $u,w\in T^-$, and let $P$ be a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$. Since $u,w\in T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} yields $E(P)\cap F\neq\emptyset$. If $e\notin E(P)$, then already $E(P)\cap F^-\neq\emptyset$. Assume now that $e\in E(P)$. Write $P=(v_0,\dots,v_m)$ and let $v_j=t$. Because $u,w\neq t$, we have $0<j<m$. Put
\[
P_u\coloneqq (v_0,\dots,v_j), \qquad P_w\coloneqq (v_j,\dots,v_m).
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:subpath-shortest}, these are shortest paths for the pairs $(u,t)$ and $(t,w)$, respectively.

Since $e$ is incident with $t$ and $t$ is an internal vertex of the path $P$, the edge $e$ is one of the two edges of $P$ incident with $t$. Hence exactly one of the two subpaths $P_u$ and $P_w$ contains $e$. Let $R$ be the other subpath. Then $R$ is a shortest path with endpoints $t$ and one of $u,w$, and $e\notin E(R)$. As both endpoints of $R$ lie in $T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} gives an edge $f\in E(R)\cap F$. Since $e\notin E(R)$, we have $f\neq e$, so $f\in F^-$. Because $E(R)\subseteq E(P)$, this shows that $E(P)\cap F^-\neq\emptyset$.

Thus every shortest path between distinct terminals of $T^-$ meets $F^-$. Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} implies that $F^-$ is semi-cutting for $T^-$.
\end{proof}

\section{A Sharp Lower Bound}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite simple undirected graph, let $T\subseteq V$, and let $F\subseteq E$. If $F$ is a semi-cutting set for $T$ in $G$, then $|F|\ge |T|-1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $(k,n)\in \mathbb{N}_0^2$, let $\mathcal{S}(k,n)$ denote the statement that every simple graph $H$ on $n$ vertices, every terminal set $X\subseteq V(H)$ with $|X|=k$, and every semi-cutting set $M\subseteq E(H)$ for $X$ satisfy $|M|\ge k-1$. We prove $\mathcal{S}(k,n)$ for all $(k,n)$ by induction on $(k,n)$ in the lexicographic order.

The cases $k\le 1$ are immediate. Fix $(k,n)$ with $k\ge 2$, assume that $\mathcal{S}(k',n')$ holds for every $(k',n')\prec (k,n)$, and let $G=(V,E)$ be a simple graph on $n$ vertices with a terminal set $T$ of size $k$ and a semi-cutting set $F\subseteq E$. We must show that $|F|\ge k-1$.

Either some terminal avoids $V(F)$, or every terminal belongs to $V(F)$.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 1: $T\setminus V(F)\neq\emptyset$.}
Choose $t\in T\setminus V(F)$. Since $k\ge 2$, pick $u\in T\setminus\{t\}$. Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} gives $\dist{G}{t}{u}<\infty$, so $N(t)\neq\emptyset$ and therefore $|N[t]|\ge 2$. Let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex, and put
\[
G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t], \qquad
T^\prime\coloneqq (T\setminus\{t\})\cup\{t^\prime\}, \qquad
F^\prime\coloneqq F/N[t].
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:contraction}, the set $F^\prime$ is semi-cutting for $T^\prime$ in $G^\prime$. Moreover, $|T^\prime|=k$ and
\[
|V(G^\prime)|=|V|-|N[t]|+1\le n-1.
\]
Hence $(|T^\prime|,|V(G^\prime)|)\prec (k,n)$, and the inductive hypothesis yields $|F^\prime|\ge k-1$. Lemma~\ref{lem:contraction-image-size} gives $|F^\prime|\le |F|$, so $|F|\ge k-1$.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 2: $T\subseteq V(F)$.}
Choose $t\in T$. Since $t\in V(F)$, some edge $e\in F$ is incident with $t$. Put $T^-\coloneqq T\setminus\{t\}$ and $F^-\coloneqq F\setminus\{e\}$. Lemma~\ref{lem:terminal-deletion} shows that $F^-$ is semi-cutting for $T^-$ in $G$. Now $|T^-|=k-1$, so $(|T^-|,|V(G)|)\prec (k,n)$. The inductive hypothesis gives $|F^-|\ge k-2$, and therefore $|F|=|F^-|+1\ge k-1$.

This completes the induction.
\end{proof}

\section{Related Problems and Further Directions}\label{sec:related}

When $|T|=2$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} shows that semi-cutting sets are exactly edge transversals of the family of geodesics between one prescribed pair of vertices. Corley and Sha study most vital links and nodes in weighted networks~\cite{corley-sha}. Malik, Mittal, and Gupta study the $k$ most vital arcs for a shortest-path problem~\cite{malik-mittal-gupta}. Nardelli, Proietti, and Widmayer study the most vital edge of a shortest path~\cite{nardelli-proietti-widmayer}. These papers are motivationally related, but they work with one distinguished source-target pair, not with a terminal set whose every pair must satisfy the strict distance increase.

For larger terminal sets, the closest classical comparison is the multiterminal cut problem studied by Dahlhaus, Johnson, Papadimitriou, Seymour, and Yannakakis~\cite{dahlhaus1994}. A multiterminal cut separates each terminal from all the others. This is stronger than the semi-cutting condition, which requires only that every terminal-terminal distance become larger. Figure~\ref{fig:not-multiterminal} illustrates the distinction: the indicated set is semi-cutting, but the terminals remain connected after the deletion.

Harary, Loukakis, and Tsouros introduced the geodetic number~\cite{harary-loukakis-tsouros}. This belongs to the same broad shortest-path background, but it is not a close analogue here: the parameter is vertex-based and global. Oellermann and Peters-Fransen study strong metric dimension for graphs and digraphs~\cite{oellermann-peters-fransen}. Our comparison with that line of work is likewise only structural, through the role played there by mutually maximally distant pairs in a vertex-covering framework.

The closest conceptual relatives seem to be the covering and packing problems for maximal shortest paths and maximal geodesics. Peterin and Semani\v{s}in study vertex covers of maximal shortest paths~\cite{peterin-semanisin}. Manuel, Bre\v{s}ar, and Klav\v{z}ar study geodesic transversals and geodesic packings for maximal geodesics~\cite{manuel-bresar-klavzar-transversal,manuel-bresar-klavzar-packing}. The present parameter differs in three respects: it is edge-based rather than vertex-based, it is restricted to the terminal-geodesic family determined by $T$, and it concerns all shortest paths between terminal pairs rather than maximal geodesics as such.

Two directions arise naturally. The first is to characterize the equality case $|F|=|T|-1$ in Theorem~\ref{thm:main}. The second is to study minimum semi-cutting sets as edge transversals of the family of shortest paths with endpoints in $T$.
