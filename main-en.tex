\author{}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite simple undirected graph, let $T\subseteq V$, and let $F\subseteq E$. We call $F$ \emph{semi-cutting} for $T$ if $\dist{G}{u}{w}<\dist{G\setminus F}{u}{w}$ for every two distinct terminals $u,w\in T$. We prove the sharp bound $|F|\ge |T|-1$. The argument is structural: semi-cutting sets are first identified with edge transversals of all shortest terminal-terminal paths, and the bound then follows from a contraction step, a terminal-deletion lemma, and a lexicographic induction on $(|T|,|V|)$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

All graphs in this paper are finite, simple, and undirected. In particular, loops and parallel edges are excluded, and every contraction is understood in the simple-graph sense: loops are deleted and parallel edges are identified. For a graph $G=(V,E)$, a terminal set $T\subseteq V$, and an edge set $F\subseteq E$, we say that $F$ is \emph{semi-cutting} for $T$ if
\[
\dist{G}{u}{w}<\dist{G\setminus F}{u}{w}
\]
for every two distinct terminals $u,w\in T$, where distances take values in $\mathbb{N}_0\cup\{\infty\}$. No global connectivity assumption is imposed on $G$. Indeed, if some terminal pair already had infinite distance in $G$, then the defining inequality would read $\infty<\infty$ and could not hold.

The condition says that every shortest path whose endpoints lie in $T$ must lose at least one edge after the deletion of $F$. A lower bound of order $|T|$ is therefore natural. It is also nontrivial: one edge may lie on shortest paths for many terminal pairs, so a direct count over pairs gives no useful control. Our main result is that every semi-cutting set satisfies $|F|\ge |T|-1$.

The bound is sharp. For $k\ge 2$, let $G$ be the path on vertices $v_0,v_1,\dots,v_{2k-2}$, let $T=\{v_0,v_2,\dots,v_{2k-2}\}$, and let $F=\bigl\{\{v_{2i},v_{2i+1}\}: 0\le i\le k-2\bigr\}$. Then $|F|=|T|-1$, and every component of $G\setminus F$ contains exactly one terminal. Hence each terminal-terminal distance is finite in $G$ and infinite in $G\setminus F$. Figure~\ref{fig:semi-cut-example} shows the case $k=3$.

Viewed through shortest paths, the problem asks for an edge transversal of the family of all shortest paths with endpoints in $T$. When $|T|=2$, this is a shortest-path disruption problem for one prescribed pair of terminals. When $|T|>2$, however, it is neither the classical multiterminal cut problem, which seeks total separation of the terminals, nor a vertex-covering problem for maximal geodesics in the whole graph. Rather, it occupies a distinct intermediate position: the family is defined metrically by terminal pairs, but the transversal is taken in edges.

The key reformulation is Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} below, which identifies semi-cutting with hitting all shortest paths whose endpoints lie in $T$ and serves as the pivot for both reductions. If a terminal $t$ is not incident with $F$, then the obstruction lies outside $N[t]$, and contracting $N[t]$ to a new terminal preserves the property while reducing the number of vertices. If every terminal meets $F$, then one deletes a terminal together with one chosen incident edge; any shortest path between the remaining terminals that uses that edge still contains another edge of $F$. This dichotomy leads to a lexicographic induction on $(|T|,|V|)$.

\section{Preliminaries}

Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite simple graph. A \emph{walk} from $u$ to $w$ is a sequence $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$ with $v_0=u$, $v_k=w$, and $\{v_{i-1},v_i\}\in E$ for $1\le i\le k$; its length is $\len{P}\coloneqq k$. A \emph{path} is a walk with pairwise distinct vertices. The distance $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is the minimum length of a $u$--$w$ path when such a path exists, and $\infty$ otherwise. A path of length $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is \emph{shortest}. For a path $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$, write $V(P)\coloneqq \{v_0,\dots,v_k\}$, $E(P)\coloneqq \bigl\{\{v_{i-1},v_i\}: 1\le i\le k\bigr\}$, and $P[v_i,v_j]\coloneqq (v_i,\dots,v_j)$ for $0\le i\le j\le k$. For $F\subseteq E$, let $G\setminus F\coloneqq (V,E\setminus F)$ and $V(F)\coloneqq \{v\in V:\ v\in e \text{ for some } e\in F\}$. For $v\in V$, let $N(v)\coloneqq \{w\in V:\ \{v,w\}\in E\}$ and $N[v]\coloneqq N(v)\cup\{v\}$.

\begin{definition}[Contraction]\label{def:contraction}
Let $\emptyset\neq W\subseteq V$, and let $w^\ast\notin V$ be a new vertex. Define $\pi_W\colon V\to (V\setminus W)\cup\{w^\ast\}$ by $\pi_W(v)=w^\ast$ for $v\in W$ and $\pi_W(v)=v$ for $v\notin W$. The contracted graph $G/W$ has vertex set $(V\setminus W)\cup\{w^\ast\}$ and edge set
\[
E(G/W)=\bigl\{\{\pi_W(x),\pi_W(y)\}: \{x,y\}\in E,\ \pi_W(x)\neq \pi_W(y)\bigr\}.
\]
Thus $G/W$ is obtained by contracting $W$ to $w^\ast$; since we remain in the category of simple graphs, loops are deleted and parallel edges are identified. If $e=\{x,y\}\in E$ and $\pi_W(x)\neq \pi_W(y)$, we write $e/W\coloneqq \{\pi_W(x),\pi_W(y)\}\in E(G/W)$. For $F\subseteq E$, we also put $F/W\coloneqq \bigl\{\{\pi_W(x),\pi_W(y)\}: \{x,y\}\in F,\ \pi_W(x)\neq \pi_W(y)\bigr\}\subseteq E(G/W)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Semi-cutting set]\label{def:semi-cut}
Let $T\subseteq V$. An edge set $F\subseteq E$ is a \emph{semi-cutting set} for $T$ in $G$ if $\dist{G}{u}{w}<\dist{G\setminus F}{u}{w}$ for every two distinct vertices $u,w\in T$.
\end{definition}

\noindent We shall use the next two standard facts about walks and shortest paths repeatedly; their proofs are standard and omitted.

\begin{lemma}[Walks can be shortened to paths]\label{lem:walk-to-path}
Every walk from $u$ to $w$ contains a $u$--$w$ path of length at most the length of the walk.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Subpaths of shortest paths]\label{lem:subpath-shortest}
If $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$ is a shortest path in $G$ and $0\le i<j\le k$, then $P[v_i,v_j]$ is a shortest path from $v_i$ to $v_j$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Projection of walks under contraction]\label{lem:projected-walk}
Let $\emptyset\neq W\subseteq V$, and let $P=(v_0,\dots,v_k)$ be a walk in $G$. Apply $\pi_W$ to each vertex of $P$ and then delete every consecutive repetition. The resulting sequence is a walk in $G/W$ from $\pi_W(v_0)$ to $\pi_W(v_k)$, and its length is at most $k$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $z_i=\pi_W(v_i)$ for $0\le i\le k$. Whenever $z_{i-1}\neq z_i$, Definition~\ref{def:contraction} gives $\{z_{i-1},z_i\}\in E(G/W)$. Deleting consecutive repetitions from $(z_0,\dots,z_k)$ therefore yields a walk in $G/W$ with the same endpoints. Only deletions are performed, so the length does not increase.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Geodesic characterization]\label{lem:geodesic-characterization}
Let $X\subseteq V$ and $M\subseteq E$. Then $M$ is a semi-cutting set for $X$ in $G$ if and only if, for every two distinct vertices $u,w\in X$, the distance $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is finite and every shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$ contains an edge of $M$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Assume first that $M$ is semi-cutting for $X$. For distinct $u,w\in X$, the distance $\dist{G}{u}{w}$ cannot be infinite, because Definition~\ref{def:semi-cut} would then give $\infty<\dist{G\setminus M}{u}{w}$. If a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$ were disjoint from $M$, it would remain a $u$--$w$ path in $G\setminus M$, contradicting the strict inequality in Definition~\ref{def:semi-cut}.

Conversely, assume that every distinct $u,w\in X$ have finite distance in $G$ and that every shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$ contains an edge of $M$. Fix such a pair $u,w$. If $u$ and $w$ are disconnected in $G\setminus M$, then $\dist{G\setminus M}{u}{w}=\infty>\dist{G}{u}{w}$. Otherwise a shortest $u$--$w$ path $Q$ in $G\setminus M$ is also a $u$--$w$ path in $G$, and equality $\len{Q}=\dist{G}{u}{w}$ is impossible because $Q$ is disjoint from $M$. Hence $\dist{G\setminus M}{u}{w}=\len{Q}>\dist{G}{u}{w}$.
\end{proof}

Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} will be used repeatedly as the geodesic reformulation of semi-cutting and is the common starting point of both reductions in the next section.

\section{Structural Reductions}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:contraction-image-size}
Let $\emptyset\neq W\subseteq V$ and let $F\subseteq E$. Then $|F/W|\le |F|$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The map $e\mapsto e/W$ from the edges of $F$ whose image is defined onto $F/W$ is surjective.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:outside-edges}
Let $t\in V$, let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex, and let $G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t]$. If $x,y\in V\setminus N[t]$ are distinct, then $\{x,y\}\in E(G^\prime)$ if and only if $\{x,y\}\in E(G)$; in that case $\{x,y\}/N[t]=\{x,y\}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $\{x,y\}\in E(G)$, then Definition~\ref{def:contraction} gives $\{x,y\}\in E(G^\prime)$ and $\{x,y\}/N[t]=\{x,y\}$. Conversely, if $\{x,y\}\in E(G^\prime)$, then some edge $\{u,v\}\in E(G)$ satisfies $\{\pi_{N[t]}(u),\pi_{N[t]}(v)\}=\{x,y\}$. Since neither endpoint is $t^\prime$, both $u$ and $v$ lie outside $N[t]$, hence $\{u,v\}=\{x,y\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:lifting-endpoint}
Let $t\in V$, let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex, and let $G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t]$. Let $z\in V\setminus N[t]$, and let $P^\prime=(t^\prime,x_1,\dots,x_k)$ be a shortest path from $t^\prime$ to $z$ in $G^\prime$. Then there exists a vertex $v\in N(t)$ such that $P=(t,v,x_1,\dots,x_k)$ is a shortest path from $t$ to $z$ in $G$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Because $P^\prime$ is a path, none of $x_1,\dots,x_k$ equals $t^\prime$; equivalently, all of them lie outside $N[t]$. The edge $\{t^\prime,x_1\}$ of $G^\prime$ therefore comes from an edge $\{v,x_1\}\in E(G)$ with $v\in N[t]$. Since $x_1\notin N[t]$, we must have $v\in N(t)$. Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges} shows that every edge $\{x_{i-1},x_i\}$ also belongs to $G$, so $P=(t,v,x_1,\dots,x_k)$ is a $t$--$z$ path in $G$ of length $\len{P^\prime}+1$.

Now let $R=(r_0,\dots,r_\ell)$ be any $t$--$z$ path in $G$. Since $z\notin N[t]$, we have $\ell\ge 2$ and $r_1\in N(t)$. Applying Lemma~\ref{lem:projected-walk} to the tail $(r_1,\dots,r_\ell)$ gives a $t^\prime$--$z$ walk in $G^\prime$ of length at most $\ell-1$, hence a $t^\prime$--$z$ path of length at most $\ell-1$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:walk-to-path}. Thus $\dist{G^\prime}{t^\prime}{z}\le \ell-1=\len{R}-1$. As $R$ was arbitrary, every $t$--$z$ path in $G$ has length at least $\dist{G^\prime}{t^\prime}{z}+1$, so $\dist{G}{t}{z}\ge \dist{G^\prime}{t^\prime}{z}+1$. Since $P$ is a $t$--$z$ path, we also have $\dist{G}{t}{z}\le \len{P}=\len{P^\prime}+1=\dist{G^\prime}{t^\prime}{z}+1$.
Hence $\len{P}=\dist{G}{t}{z}$, and $P$ is shortest.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:contraction}
Let $T\subseteq V$ and $F\subseteq E$. Assume that $F$ is a semi-cutting set for $T$ in $G$. Let $t\in T\setminus V(F)$ and let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex. Put $G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t]$, $T^\prime\coloneqq (T\setminus\{t\})\cup\{t^\prime\}$, and $F^\prime\coloneqq F/N[t]$. Then $F^\prime$ is a semi-cutting set for $T^\prime$ in $G^\prime$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
No terminal of $T\setminus\{t\}$ lies in $N[t]$. Indeed, if $u\in T\setminus\{t\}$ belonged to $N[t]$, then $\{t,u\}$ would be the unique shortest $t$--$u$ path in $G$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} would force $\{t,u\}\in F$, contradicting $t\notin V(F)$. Thus $T\setminus\{t\}\subseteq V\setminus N[t]$. In particular, every terminal in $T\setminus\{t\}$ survives the contraction as the same vertex, no two terminals are identified, and $T^\prime$ is well defined.

Every two distinct terminals of $T^\prime$ have finite distance in $G^\prime$. If $u,w\in T\setminus\{t\}$, take a $u$--$w$ path in $G$; if one terminal is $t^\prime$ and the other is $z\in T\setminus\{t\}$, take a $t$--$z$ path in $G$. In both cases, Lemmas~\ref{lem:projected-walk} and~\ref{lem:walk-to-path} produce a path in $G^\prime$ joining the corresponding terminals. By Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization}, it remains to show that every shortest path in $G^\prime$ between distinct terminals of $T^\prime$ meets $F^\prime$.

Let $u,w\in T^\prime$ be distinct, and let $P^\prime$ be a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G^\prime$.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 1: $t^\prime\notin V(P^\prime)$.}
Then every vertex of $P^\prime$ lies outside $N[t]$, so $P^\prime$ is also a path in $G$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges}. It is shortest in $G$. Indeed, if $R$ were a shorter $u$--$w$ path in $G$, then Lemma~\ref{lem:projected-walk} would project $R$ to a $u$--$w$ walk in $G^\prime$ of length at most $\len{R}<\len{P^\prime}$. Shortening that walk to a path by Lemma~\ref{lem:walk-to-path} would still give a $u$--$w$ path in $G^\prime$ of length strictly smaller than $\len{P^\prime}$, contradicting the choice of $P^\prime$. Since now $u,w\in T\setminus\{t\}\subseteq T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} yields an edge $g\in E(P^\prime)\cap F$. Both ends of $g$ lie outside $N[t]$, hence $g/N[t]=g\in F^\prime$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges}.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 2: $t^\prime\in V(P^\prime)$.}
Because $P^\prime$ is a path, the vertex $t^\prime$ appears on it at most once. By symmetry, write $P^\prime=(y_0,\dots,y_m)$ with $y_r=t^\prime$ for some $r<m$. Put $z\coloneqq y_m\in T^\prime\setminus\{t^\prime\}=T\setminus\{t\}$ and $Q^\prime\coloneqq P^\prime[y_r,y_m]$. Since $Q^\prime$ is a path, all vertices $y_{r+1},\dots,y_m$ lie outside $N[t]$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:subpath-shortest}, $Q^\prime$ is a shortest $t^\prime$--$z$ path in $G^\prime$. Lemma~\ref{lem:lifting-endpoint} therefore provides a vertex $v\in N(t)$ such that $Q=(t,v,y_{r+1},\dots,y_m)$ is a shortest $t$--$z$ path in $G$; see Figure~\ref{fig:contraction}.

Since $t,z\in T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} gives $E(Q)\cap F\neq\emptyset$. The first edge $\{t,v\}$ is not in $F$ because $t\notin V(F)$. Hence some edge $f\in E(Q)\cap F$ lies after the first step of $Q$. If $f=\{v,y_{r+1}\}$, then $f/N[t]=\{t^\prime,y_{r+1}\}$ is the first edge of $Q^\prime$. Otherwise $f=\{y_i,y_{i+1}\}$ for some $i\in\{r+1,\dots,m-1\}$, and both endpoints of $f$ lie outside $N[t]$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:outside-edges} gives $f/N[t]=f\in E(Q^\prime)$. Because $G^\prime$ is simple, distinct edges of $G$ may acquire the same image after contracting $N[t]$; however, the definition of $F^\prime=F/N[t]$ guarantees that any edge of $G^\prime$ which is the image of some edge of $F$ belongs to $F^\prime$, regardless of any additional preimages outside $F$. Thus the image of $f$ lies in $E(Q^\prime)\cap F^\prime\subseteq E(P^\prime)\cap F^\prime$.

Thus every shortest path in $G^\prime$ between distinct terminals of $T^\prime$ meets $F^\prime$. Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Deleting one terminal together with one incident edge]\label{lem:terminal-deletion}
Let $T\subseteq V$ and $F\subseteq E$. Assume that $F$ is a semi-cutting set for $T$ in $G$. Let $t\in T$, and let $e\in F$ be an edge incident with $t$. Put $T^-\coloneqq T\setminus\{t\}$ and $F^-\coloneqq F\setminus\{e\}$. Then $F^-$ is a semi-cutting set for $T^-$ in $G$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $|T^-|\le 1$, there is nothing to prove. Assume $|T^-|\ge 2$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization}, every two distinct vertices of $T^-$ have finite distance in $G$. It is therefore enough to show that every shortest path between two distinct vertices of $T^-$ contains an edge of $F^-$.

Take distinct $u,w\in T^-$, and let $P$ be a shortest $u$--$w$ path in $G$. Since $u,w\in T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} yields $E(P)\cap F\neq\emptyset$. If $e\notin E(P)$, then already $E(P)\cap F^-\neq\emptyset$. Assume now that $e\in E(P)$. Write $P=(v_0,\dots,v_m)$ and let $v_j=t$. Because $u,w\neq t$, we have $0<j<m$. Put $P_u\coloneqq (v_0,\dots,v_j)$ and $P_w\coloneqq (v_j,\dots,v_m)$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:subpath-shortest}, these are shortest paths for the pairs $(u,t)$ and $(t,w)$, respectively.

Since $e$ is incident with $t$ and $t$ is an internal vertex of the path $P$, the edge $e$ lies on exactly one of $P_u$ and $P_w$; thus the choice of the other subpath is unambiguous. Let $R$ denote that other subpath. Then $R$ is a shortest path with endpoints $t$ and one of $u,w$, and $e\notin E(R)$. As both endpoints of $R$ lie in $T$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} gives an edge $f\in E(R)\cap F$. Since $e\notin E(R)$, we have $f\neq e$, so $f\in F^-$. Because $E(R)\subseteq E(P)$, the path $P$ contains an edge of $F^-$. Therefore every shortest path between distinct terminals of $T^-$ meets $F^-$, and Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} yields the claim.
\end{proof}

\section{A Sharp Lower Bound}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite simple undirected graph, let $T\subseteq V$, and let $F\subseteq E$. If $F$ is a semi-cutting set for $T$ in $G$, then $|F|\ge |T|-1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $(k,n)\in \mathbb{N}_0^2$, let $\mathcal{S}(k,n)$ denote the statement that every simple graph $H$ on $n$ vertices, every terminal set $X\subseteq V(H)$ with $|X|=k$, and every semi-cutting set $M\subseteq E(H)$ for $X$ satisfy $|M|\ge k-1$. We prove $\mathcal{S}(k,n)$ for all $(k,n)$ by induction on $(k,n)$ in the lexicographic order.

The statement is vacuous when $k\le 1$. Fix $(k,n)$ with $k\ge 2$, assume that $\mathcal{S}(k',n')$ holds for every $(k',n')\prec (k,n)$, and let $G=(V,E)$ be a simple graph on $n$ vertices with a terminal set $T$ of size $k$ and a semi-cutting set $F\subseteq E$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization}, every terminal pair in $T$ has finite distance in $G$, so both reductions below are metrically meaningful even without any assumption that $G$ be connected.

Either some terminal avoids $V(F)$, or every terminal belongs to $V(F)$; these two cases are disjoint and exhaustive.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 1: $T\setminus V(F)\neq\emptyset$.}
Choose $t\in T\setminus V(F)$. Since $k\ge 2$, pick $u\in T\setminus\{t\}$. Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} gives $\dist{G}{t}{u}<\infty$, so $N(t)\neq\emptyset$ and therefore $|N[t]|\ge 2$. Let $t^\prime\notin V$ be a new vertex, and set $G^\prime\coloneqq G/N[t]$, $T^\prime\coloneqq (T\setminus\{t\})\cup\{t^\prime\}$, and $F^\prime\coloneqq F/N[t]$. By Proposition~\ref{prop:contraction}, the set $F^\prime$ is semi-cutting for $T^\prime$ in $G^\prime$. Moreover, $|T^\prime|=k$ and $|V(G^\prime)|=|V|-|N[t]|+1\le n-1$.
Hence $(|T^\prime|,|V(G^\prime)|)\prec (k,n)$, so $|F^\prime|\ge k-1$ by induction. Lemma~\ref{lem:contraction-image-size} gives $|F^\prime|\le |F|$, and therefore $|F|\ge k-1$.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Case 2: $T\subseteq V(F)$.}
Choose $t\in T$. Since $t\in V(F)$, some edge $e\in F$ is incident with $t$. Put $T^-\coloneqq T\setminus\{t\}$ and $F^-\coloneqq F\setminus\{e\}$. Lemma~\ref{lem:terminal-deletion} shows that $F^-$ is semi-cutting for $T^-$ in $G$. As $(|T^-|,|V(G)|)\prec (k,n)$, induction gives $|F^-|\ge k-2$, and therefore $|F|=|F^-|+1\ge k-1$.

Together, the contraction step in Case~1 and the terminal-deletion step in Case~2 close the induction and prove the lower bound.
\end{proof}

\section{Related Problems and Further Directions}\label{sec:related}

When $|T|=2$, Lemma~\ref{lem:geodesic-characterization} turns semi-cutting into the problem of hitting all shortest paths between one prescribed pair of terminals. This places the parameter near shortest-path disruption and most-vital-edge questions. Corley and Sha, and also Malik, Mittal, and Gupta, study the deletion of a prescribed number of edges, vertices, or arcs so as to maximize the increase of the shortest distance between one fixed source-target pair in a weighted network~\cite{corley-sha,malik-mittal-gupta}, while Nardelli, Proietti, and Widmayer analyze the most vital edge of a shortest source-target path together with the corresponding replacement shortest paths after removing one edge of that path~\cite{nardelli-proietti-widmayer}. Our parameter is different even in this two-terminal regime: it does not optimize a distance increase, but asks whether a given edge set meets \emph{all} shortest paths between the prescribed terminals; for larger $T$, the relevant family simultaneously includes the shortest paths for every terminal pair.

For larger terminal sets, the closest classical separation problem is the multiterminal cut problem of Dahlhaus, Johnson, Papadimitriou, Seymour, and Yannakakis~\cite{dahlhaus1994}. There the aim is to delete edges so that each terminal is separated from all the others, and isolating cuts and multiterminal cuts are defined precisely in that sense. Semi-cutting imposes a strictly weaker topological requirement but a subtler metric one: every terminal-terminal distance must increase, yet the terminals may remain in the same connected component after deleting $F$. Figure~\ref{fig:not-multiterminal} exhibits exactly this phenomenon, showing that semi-cutting is not reducible to multiterminal separation.

The closest conceptual neighbors are vertex parameters defined by geodesic families. Harary, Loukakis, and Tsouros introduce geodetic closure and the geodetic number~\cite{harary-loukakis-tsouros}, while Oellermann and Peters-Fransen use intervals $I[u,v]$ in their study of strong metric dimension~\cite{oellermann-peters-fransen}; both belong to the broader tradition of selecting vertices through shortest-path structure. Closer to the present setting, Peterin and Semani\v{s}in study a minimum vertex set meeting all maximal shortest paths~\cite{peterin-semanisin}, and Manuel, Bre\v{s}ar, and Klav\v{z}ar define the geodesic-transversal number as a minimum vertex set hitting all maximal geodesics and later introduce the dual geodesic packing parameter~\cite{manuel-bresar-klavzar-transversal,manuel-bresar-klavzar-packing}. Semi-cutting differs from this line in three ways: it is edge-based rather than vertex-based, it is restricted to the terminal-defined family of shortest paths, and it requires hitting all shortest terminal-terminal paths rather than all maximal geodesics of the graph.

Two problems arise naturally from Theorem~\ref{thm:main}. One is to characterize the equality case $|F|=|T|-1$. The other is to study minimum semi-cutting sets as edge transversals of the family of shortest paths with endpoints in $T$.

Theorem~\ref{thm:main} thus isolates a structural phenomenon that is independent of algorithmic considerations: once semi-cutting is viewed through the restricted geodesic family determined by $T$, contraction and terminal deletion force a universal lower bound. Further progress therefore seems likely to come from structural characterizations of extremal configurations rather than from procedures.
